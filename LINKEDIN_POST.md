## Version 1, The Story (medium length, reflective)

When I moved from software development into middle school computer science, I was worried I'd miss building things. What I've learned is that teaching is building, just with kids and code at the same time.

This spring my students and I built **Care Garden**, an interactive PBIS installation for our school lobby. Students contribute acts of kindness through a simple kiosk, and each one blooms as a unique flower in a shared digital garden on a big display.

Over the course of one week, thousands of contributions came in. Watching the garden fill up, watching students pull their friends over to find "their" flower, watching what code can do when it's framed as an excuse to be kind, was genuinely one of the most rewarding things I've done as a teacher.

It started as a creative-coding unit in p5.js and grew into a piece of school culture. That's the kind of work I want to keep doing, projects that sit at the intersection of education, design, and code, and that leave a school better than they found it.

Happy to talk to anyone working on similar things in ed-tech, museums, or instructional design.

## Version 3, The Reflective One (longer, more personal)

A few years ago I left software development to teach middle school computer science. People kept asking me if I missed shipping real products. For a while, I wasn't sure how to answer.

Then we built **Care Garden**.

It started as a creative-coding unit in p5.js, my way of showing students that code can be a medium for expression, not just a tool for solving problems. By the end of the unit, a few of them asked what else we could make together. That question turned into a PBIS installation in our school lobby: a shared digital garden where every act of kindness students submitted bloomed as a unique flower on a big display.

We ran it for a week.

Thousands of contributions came in. Students crowded around the kiosk between classes. Teachers stopped to add their own flower. Kids pulled their friends over to point out the one they'd made. For a week, the lobby felt different, slower, warmer, more alive.

I don't miss shipping products. What I was really doing back then, and what I'm still doing now, is building things that change how people experience a space. The stakes are different in a middle school. The users are more honest. The feedback is more immediate. And the work is, somehow, more real.

I'm looking for my next chapter, instructional design, experience design, or ed-tech roles where I can keep doing this kind of work at a larger scale. If that's something you're building, I'd love to talk.

## Tips for posting

- **Pair with visuals**: attach 2, 3 of the care-garden photos you already have, LinkedIn loves image posts and they'll outperform text-only.
- **Pin this post** on your profile, so recruiters see it first when they land on your page.
- **Tag folks** who should see it: Anthropic colleagues who've seen the work, your school admin (if you want), or any contacts at museums / ed-tech companies you want to spark a conversation with.
- **Link to your site** at the end (once it's live) so there's a next step for anyone interested.
- **Post on a Tuesday morning** (9, 11am local), that's when LinkedIn engagement peaks.
